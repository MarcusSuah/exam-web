Executive Summary

The purpose of this study proposal is to examine the factors that lead to exam-cheating behavior among students as well as the potential efficacy of Safe Exam Browser in doing so. It will examine the applicability and significance of the study, the aim of the research plan, and the summary, conclusions, and suggestions. The study results could be used by educational institutions to create strategies and policies to discourage exam-taking fraud and support student academic integrity. Previous studies have demonstrated that students cheat for a variety of causes, including the pressure to perform well, a lack of planning, and the belief that cheating is okay. Many strategies, including proctors, invigilators, and monitoring tools, have been used to stop cheating.
An open-source web browser called Safe Exam Browser was created to stop cheating on online tests. This review will look at how frequently students cheat on exams, what influences student cheating, why students cheat, and how well safeguards like the safe test browser work. Additionally, it will examine how technology is used, how lecturers and students view the use of secure test browsers, and the various forms of cheating behavior, including collusion, plagiarism, and cheating on exams.

About

SEB runs on a local computer and it is connected via the internet to a learning management system (LMS) or an e-assessment system. Generally SEB works with any web based LMS and other kinds of web based exam systems. Some learning management systems (LMS) like for example Moodle, ILIAS, OpenOlat and exam solutions as Inspera Assessment offer a quiz mode specifically compatible with SEB.

SEB does not connect to any central server or cloud service! SEB Server is being developed as an independent open source software component that can be installed on a self-operated server. SEB clients can then be connected to such an SEB server instance using individual configuration files. This is strictly optional and must be configured individually in any case. This also applies to the support and proctoring capabilities in new SEB versions via video conferencing services such as Jitsi Meet (open source, can be installed on a self-operated server) or Zoom (commercial, cloud-based). This video conferencing connection must also be configured individually and is always disabled by default. If an SEB client has been configured to connect to a video conferencing service or an SEB server instance, this will be indicated to candidates when starting an exam session and video/audio proctoring/support must be explicitly agreed to by each exam candidate in a dialog box.
